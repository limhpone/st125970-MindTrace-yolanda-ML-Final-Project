\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Data Source}

This study utilises the publicly available Emotion Dataset from Kaggle~\citep{emotions_kaggle}, a large-scale collection of Twitter posts annotated into six discrete emotion categories: anger, joy, sadness, fear, surprise, and love. The dataset comprises 416,809 labelled text samples, making it statistically robust for supervised multi-class classification modelling.

The dataset is methodologically justified on three grounds. First, its large sample size enhances statistical reliability and reduces variance in model estimation. Second, it reflects informal, real-world social media language --- including abbreviations, emotive expressions, and conversational tone --- thereby increasing ecological validity. Third, its six-class taxonomy enables fine-grained emotional analysis beyond binary sentiment polarity, directly aligning with the research objective of multi-class emotion prediction.

\subsubsection{Correlation Analysis}

A Pearson correlation matrix (Figure~\ref{fig:correlation}) was computed to examine linear relationships among numerical features. A strong positive correlation ($r = 0.984$) was observed between \texttt{text\_length} and \texttt{word\_count}, confirming structural redundancy --- only one need be retained as a feature. In contrast, the correlation between the numeric label encoding and length-based features is near-zero ($r < 0.03$), confirming that emotional classification depends primarily on semantic and contextual content rather than message length. This reinforces TF-IDF and embedding-based representations as the essential feature extraction strategy.

\subsection{Proposed Architecture}

The proposed methodology follows a structured NLP workflow: preprocessing $\rightarrow$ feature extraction $\rightarrow$ model development $\rightarrow$ evaluation $\rightarrow$ deployment. The full pipeline is illustrated in Figures~\ref{fig:process_flow} and~\ref{fig:pipeline}.

\textbf{Preprocessing.} Text normalisation includes lowercasing, URL and special character removal, stopword elimination, and lemmatisation. Informal abbreviations and chat words are standardised to reduce lexical noise. Stopword removal uses the NLTK English stopword list; however, negation words and intensifiers (\eg, ``not'', ``never'', ``very'', ``extremely'') are retained via a custom exception list, as these tokens carry direct emotional polarity and their removal would cause loss of discriminative signal. Class imbalance is addressed through stratified sampling and class-weighted learning, with SMOTE resampling considered as an alternative strategy.

\textbf{Feature Representation.} For machine learning models, TF-IDF vectorisation transforms text into numerical feature representations:
\begin{equation}
\text{TF-IDF}(t, d) = \text{TF}(t, d) \times \log\!\left(\frac{N}{\text{DF}(t)}\right)
\label{eq:tfidf}
\end{equation}
where $\text{TF}(t,d)$ is the term frequency, $\text{DF}(t)$ is the document frequency, and $N$ is the total number of documents. For deep learning models, an embedding layer converts tokens into dense vector representations.

\textbf{Data Splitting.} The dataset is split 80\% training / 20\% testing with a 20\% validation holdout from training data, using stratified sampling to preserve class proportions.

\textbf{Evaluation Metrics.} Model performance is evaluated using:
\begin{equation}
\text{Accuracy} = \frac{TP + TN}{TP + TN + FP + FN}
\label{eq:accuracy}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
F_1 = \frac{2 \times \text{Precision} \times \text{Recall}}{\text{Precision} + \text{Recall}}
\label{eq:f1}
\end{equation}
