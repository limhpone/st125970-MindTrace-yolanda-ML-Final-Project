\section{Experimental Results and Discussion}
\label{sec:results}

All four models were trained and evaluated on the preprocessed, class-balanced dataset (89,232 samples; $\approx$14,872 per class after downsampling) using an 80/20 stratified train-test split with a 20\% validation holdout from training data. The test set comprised 17,967 samples.

\subsection{Hyperparameter Tuning Strategy}

To ensure a fair comparison, all models were tuned using systematic and reproducible strategies appropriate to each architecture family.

\textbf{SVM.} Grid search with 3-fold cross-validation was conducted over the regularisation parameter $C \in \{0.1, 1\}$ and kernel type $\in$ \{linear, rbf, poly\}. The best configuration was $C = 1$ with a linear kernel, achieving 92.08\% validation accuracy. The linear kernel's superiority over RBF and polynomial kernels is consistent with the high-dimensional, sparse nature of TF-IDF representations, where linear separability often holds~\citep{dzikovska2012using}.

\textbf{XGBoost.} Grid search with 3-fold cross-validation explored learning rate $\in \{0.01, 0.1\}$, maximum tree depth $\in \{5, 10\}$, and number of estimators $\in \{50, 100\}$. The optimal configuration was learning\_rate $= 0.1$, max\_depth $= 10$, and $n\_$estimators $= 100$, achieving 90.98\% cross-validated accuracy. The preference for deeper trees and higher learning rate suggests that the emotion classification task requires substantial non-linear modelling capacity.

\textbf{BiLSTM.} Architecture hyperparameters were set based on established practices for text classification~\citep{sun2023bidirectional}: embedding dimension $= 100$, two stacked BiLSTM layers ($256 \rightarrow 128$ units), dropout rate $= 0.5$ between dense layers, batch size $= 32$, and Adam optimiser with default learning rate ($10^{-3}$). EarlyStopping with patience $= 3$ (monitoring validation loss, restoring best weights) served as the primary regularisation mechanism, halting training at epoch~7 with best weights from epoch~4.

\textbf{CNN.} Two convolutional layers ($256 \rightarrow 128$ filters, kernel size $= 5$) were used, each followed by max-pooling, with a dense layer and dropout ($= 0.5$). The same EarlyStopping configuration was applied with a fresh callback instance, terminating training at epoch~3. The embedding dimension was set to 100, consistent with BiLSTM, to ensure comparable feature representations.

\subsection{Confusion Matrix Analysis}

Figures~\ref{fig:cm_bilstm}--\ref{fig:cm_xgboost} present the confusion matrices for all four models. Two dominant misclassification patterns emerged consistently:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Joy $\leftrightarrow$ Love confusion.} All models misclassified Joy samples as Love (BiLSTM: 236, CNN: 255, SVM: 258, XGBoost: 246), reflecting semantic proximity between happiness and affection.
    \item \textbf{Fear $\rightarrow$ Surprise confusion.} Fear samples were frequently predicted as Surprise (BiLSTM: 269, CNN: 278, SVM: 275, XGBoost: 303), consistent with shared vocabulary of unexpected or alarming events.
\end{enumerate}

Love and Surprise were the most accurately classified categories, with BiLSTM achieving 99.8\% per-class recall on both. XGBoost exhibited a distinctive failure mode: Sadness$\rightarrow$Joy (852), Anger$\rightarrow$Joy (650), and Fear$\rightarrow$Joy (531) misclassifications, indicating susceptibility to majority-class bias.

\subsection{Error Analysis and Potential Biases}

\subsubsection{Systematic Error Patterns}

The confusion matrices reveal three systematic biases that persist across all architectures:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Valence conflation.} Joy$\leftrightarrow$Love confusion arises because both categories share positively-valenced vocabulary (``happy'', ``wonderful'', ``amazing''). Models trained on bag-of-words or local n-gram features cannot reliably distinguish hedonic pleasure (Joy) from relational attachment (Love) without deeper pragmatic reasoning.
    \item \textbf{Arousal ambiguity.} Fear$\rightarrow$Surprise errors stem from shared high-arousal lexicon (``shocked'', ``suddenly'', ``unexpected''). The two emotions differ primarily in valence (negative vs.\ neutral/positive), a distinction that requires contextual inference beyond surface-level token co-occurrence.
    \item \textbf{Majority-class gravity (XGBoost).} XGBoost's pronounced bias toward Joy predictions (852 Sadness, 650 Anger, 531 Fear misclassified as Joy) suggests that despite class-balanced training, the tree-based ensemble learned Joy-associated splits as default decision paths, likely due to high TF-IDF feature overlap between negatively-valenced classes and the dominant positive class.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Dataset Bias}

Several dataset-level biases should be acknowledged. First, the corpus is drawn exclusively from Twitter, introducing \textbf{platform bias}: the informal, character-limited style of tweets may not generalise to longer-form text in domains such as product reviews, clinical notes, or news articles. Second, \textbf{annotation bias} is inherent in crowd-sourced emotion labels, as annotator agreement on ambiguous texts (e.g., sarcastic posts) is typically moderate~\citep{demszky2020goemotions}. Third, \textbf{temporal bias} arises because the dataset captures language patterns from a specific time period; evolving slang, emoji usage, and cultural expression may reduce model performance on future data without periodic retraining. Fourth, the six-class emotion taxonomy imposes a \textbf{label granularity constraint}: real-world emotional expression is continuous and often multi-label (e.g., bittersweet posts combining Joy and Sadness), which the current single-label framework cannot capture.

\subsection{Interpretability and Feasibility}

The interpretability of each model varies substantially. SVM offers \textbf{high interpretability}: the linear decision function $f(x) = w \cdot x + b$ provides direct feature importance through the weight vector $w$, enabling verification that predictions rely on semantically meaningful tokens --- essential for clinical and mental health applications where decision auditability is required. XGBoost offers \textbf{moderate interpretability} through built-in feature importance scores (gain, cover, frequency), though interaction effects across hundreds of trees are difficult to trace. CNN and BiLSTM are \textbf{low interpretability} black-box classifiers; future work will integrate SHAP and LIME for model-agnostic explanations.

\subsection{Deployment and User Interface}

SVM was selected as the production model due to its strong accuracy (91.80\%), sub-millisecond inference, and interpretable decision function. The trained SVM classifier and TF-IDF vectoriser were serialised using \texttt{joblib} and integrated into a Flask-based REST API, containerised with Docker (\texttt{python:3.11-slim}), and served via Gunicorn.

The web interface provides: (1)~a text input field returning the predicted emotion with class-wise probability scores as an interactive bar chart; (2)~a preprocessing trace panel displaying each NLP transformation applied to the input; and (3)~a model performance dashboard with accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score from training evaluation. The Docker image (\texttt{yolandalim/125970-mindtrace:v2}) is publicly available, ensuring full reproducibility.

\subsection{Summary of Key Findings}

The experimental evaluation yielded five principal findings:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{BiLSTM dominates.} Bidirectional contextual encoding achieved the highest performance (94.78\% accuracy, 0.9477 macro-F1), confirming the advantage of sequential modelling for emotion classification.
    \item \textbf{SVM is the most efficient.} SVM delivered 91.80\% accuracy at a fraction of BiLSTM's computational cost, making it the optimal choice for latency-sensitive deployment.
    \item \textbf{XGBoost underperforms.} Tree-based ensembles struggle with high-dimensional TF-IDF features, achieving only 80.93\% accuracy and exhibiting strong majority-class bias toward Joy.
    \item \textbf{Semantic overlap is the bottleneck.} Joy$\leftrightarrow$Love and Fear$\rightarrow$Surprise confusion persisted across all architectures, indicating that the misclassification boundary is defined by emotional semantics rather than model capacity.
    \item \textbf{Dataset and annotation biases exist.} Platform-specific language, crowd-sourced labelling noise, and temporal drift constrain the generalisability of all results to the Twitter domain.
\end{enumerate}
