\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This proposal has argued that the dominant limitation in current emotion classification research is not model capability but experimental inconsistency --- studies differ in dataset, preprocessing, and evaluation metric, making performance comparisons unreliable. MindTrace addresses this directly by fixing all experimental conditions and varying only the model, isolating intrinsic differences between SVM, XGBoost, CNN, and BiLSTM on a 416,809-sample Twitter corpus.

The EDA presented in Section~\ref{sec:methodology} confirms that the dataset is structurally sound, class-imbalanced at a ratio of approximately 9.5\,:\,1, and that semantic overlap between adjacent emotion categories (fear/surprise, sadness/love) will be the primary challenge for all models. The architectural specifications in Section~\ref{sec:algorithms} ensure the study is fully reproducible.

If the comparative results confirm that BiLSTM outperforms classical baselines on minority emotion classes under macro-F1 --- as the literature suggests --- this will provide concrete, dataset-specific evidence that deep contextual architectures are worth the additional computational cost for emotion-aware applications in social media analytics, mental health monitoring, and human-computer interaction.

Future work will extend the framework in four directions:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item Fine-tuning transformer-based models (BERT, RoBERTa) on the same dataset to compare against the baselines established here.
    \item Cross-domain evaluation extending the pipeline to non-Twitter corpora such as Reddit or product reviews to assess generalisation.
    \item Real-time deployment as a production REST API with a web-based emotion monitoring dashboard.
    \item Explainability analysis using SHAP and LIME to identify the token-level features that most discriminate between semantically similar emotion classes.
\end{enumerate}
