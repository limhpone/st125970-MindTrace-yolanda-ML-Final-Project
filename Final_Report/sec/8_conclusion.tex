\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This study presented MindTrace, a unified benchmarking framework for multi-class emotion classification, systematically comparing SVM, XGBoost, CNN, and BiLSTM on a 416,809-sample Twitter dataset under consistent preprocessing, stratified splits, and macro-F1 evaluation.

For \textbf{RQ1}, BiLSTM achieved the highest performance (94.70\% accuracy, macro-F1 0.9464), followed by CNN (93.13\%), SVM (91.80\%), and XGBoost (80.93\%), highlighting the effectiveness of bidirectional contextual modelling over bag-of-words and local feature approaches.

For \textbf{RQ2}, confusion matrix analysis revealed consistent misclassification patterns (Joy$\leftrightarrow$Love and Fear$\rightarrow$Surprise), suggesting that semantic overlap between emotion categories, rather than model capacity alone, is a key contributor to residual classification errors.

For \textbf{RQ3}, SVM was selected for deployment due to its ${\sim}23\times$ lower inference latency while maintaining competitive performance. The model was implemented as a Flask REST API with Docker and CI/CD, demonstrating that classical TF-IDF-based approaches can provide efficient and reliable real-time emotion prediction. Ablation studies further identified stopword removal ($-1.33$\,pp) and BiLSTM depth ($-1.22$\,pp) as the most impactful components.

Despite these contributions, several limitations remain. The evaluation relies on a single stratified train--test split for final comparison, while cross-validation (3-fold) was applied during hyperparameter tuning for machine learning models. Full cross-validation across all architectures was not performed due to computational constraints, particularly for deep learning models, and to preserve consistent experimental conditions across model families. Consequently, the reported performance differences may be influenced by split-specific variance. In addition, class imbalance was addressed through random downsampling, potentially discarding informative samples. The use of a Twitter-only dataset and constrained hyperparameter tuning further limit generalisability, and transformer-based architectures were not included.

Future work should incorporate cross-validation and statistical significance testing, evaluate transformer-based architectures alongside additional baselines, and explore alternative imbalance handling strategies. Extending the framework to cross-domain datasets, enhancing preprocessing techniques (\eg, emoji normalisation and intensity modelling), and integrating interpretability methods such as SHAP and LIME will further improve robustness and applicability.
